Character Progression: Architectures

Traditional MMORPG-style progression metrics (levels, skill points, loot, credits, reputation, etc) exist, but they only scratch the surface of how individual pilots progress.

Health and durability are expressed through redundant systems and backup processes. Even if an avatar's skill-chosen weapon or armor module goes offline, alternate systems can compensate.

Player avatars are tied to localized sub-routine "SHELLS" rather than corporeal bodies. Think of each avatar as operating within a mini-neural AI housed in virtual or functional vessel, able to transfer consciousness or core data to backups.

In practice: - Hacking avatars have multiple "memory backups" (checkpoint saves) on friendly subsystems. - Death in the data layer only indicates a reset to the last backup, not total loss.

In essence, each character's progression involves optimizing this survival redundancy alongside traditional gains.

Cycle-Time: Universal Currency

Definition:

One Cycle-Time (CT) represents one second of baseline computation in the Matrioshka brain. Nearly all economic transactions, from ship repairs to sub-routine wages, are denominated in CT.

Philosophical Foundation:

Defines time as the fundamental commodity in a post-human society. Players trade processing cycles just as previous societies traded gold or oil.

Active Generation:

Players can increase CT income by:

  • Deploying data-mining subroutines on idle CPU cores
  • Completing digital contracts in the brain's fabric

Mission Rewards:

  • High-level missions pay bonuses in CT for faster progress.

Resource Sale:

  • Certain rare minerals can be sold for bulk CT at processing hubs.

Convoy Contracts:

  • Transportation of core computation (Cycle-Time delivery) itself becomes a lucrative trade.

Ship Construction/Repair:

  • All ship building and repair costs are calculated in CT.

Sub-Routine Maintenance:

  • Maintaining advanced subroutines (like automated fleets) incurs regular CT taxes.

Instruction Set Unlocks:

  • Learning new skills or activation of advanced modules requires CT investment.

Docking Fees & Services:

  • Space station docking and luxury amenities are priced in Cycle-Time.

The Crew System: Sub-Routines

Each ship is managed by a small multi-role crew (the "Core Crew"), plus a pool of remote subroutines. The Core Crew has three main focus roles:

  • Pilots control ship navigation and movement
  • Gunners manage primary weapon systems
  • Engineers handle shields, repairs, and power distribution

Additional crew positions (the sub-routine pool) can be filled by any player who opts out of a Core role:

  • Auxiliary Gunners can operate extra turrets or weapon ports
  • Support Specialists handle repair drones and emergency systems
  • Advanced Modules player-created subroutines for automated tasks (like mining or scanning)

All crew-members share earned Experience Points, but equip separate Role Modules:

  • Gunslinger Module increases direct damage output
  • Pilot Augmentation Module improves ship handling and sensor range
  • Tech Support Module boosts repair rates and shield stability

This system encourages players to switch roles mid-battle if needed (e.g. an Engineer jumps to the turret when under heavy fire, then switches back when repairs are done).

Voxel Destruction: Modular Ship Architecture

All space combat damage is handled by a voxel damage model. Each ship is made of discrete modules (voxels) with specific subsystems:

Core Block Types:

  • Processor Cores (Power & Control)
  • Armor Plating (Defense, impacts durability)
  • Weapon Blocks (Turrets, cannons, missile bays)
  • Engine Modules (Propulsion & maneuvering)
  • Reactor Chambers (Energy generation)
  • Coolant Tanks (Heat management)
  • Buffer Arrays (Cargo & data redundancy)

Damage is localized to voxel blocks. For example, hitting a Engine Module disables propulsion in that section, but other engines can compensate if power is re-routed.

Destruction Chain: If enough blocks of a subsystem are destroyed, the subsystem goes offline entirely. Players can target specific blocks to disable shields or engines. This creates emergent tactics like firing through weaker asteroid belts to tunnel-shot critical parts of a ship.
